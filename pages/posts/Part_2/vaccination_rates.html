Do Vaccination Rates Impact Flu Rates?¶

Flu shots are important, as any doctor will tell you. But can we use our data and statistics to explore how flu shots impact the flu?

In this exercise, we'll examine US flu and flu shot data to see if there are any meaningful conclusions. Along the way, we'll use analytical SQL to find the year-over-year change in certain data. We'll also build some simple Ordinary Least Squares regression models models to evaluate the significance of our assumptions.

To begin, start with a typical set of imports and the addition of the statsmodel Python library.

Getting the Data¶

We'll begin by looking at our flu shot data. The flu shot data is by state and the US flu surveillance data is by region, so we'll have to join with the state_stats table to map states to regions. From there, we can determine a few more things:

  • the number of elegible Medicare recipients in the region
  • the average percentage of Medicare recipients to receive a flu shot in the region
  • the year of the observation

In order to determine the impact of flu shots on the flu, we need to see if something changed. That "something" is a response variable. In our case, the response variable is the year-to-year change in type-A flu cases.

Our data only records totals, not the changes between them. Fortunately, many databases include a lag function which allows us to compare a record to the one prior to it. Let's use a SQL lag function to find year-over-year change in type-A flu.

In [4]: 
%%sql select total_a - lag(total_a) over (order by year), region, year
      from (
        select sum(a_total) as total_a, sum(b) as total_b, region, year
        from us_who_flu_stats 
        where year in (2012,2013,2014) group by year, region
       )

We could merge these two DataFrames together in python, but we can get a single DataFrame to work with by writing a SQL clause which joins the results. Notice that each of our previous queries is now a subquery.

%%sql with shots as (
  select sum(eligible) as all_eligible, avg(percentage_claimed) as vaccinated, region_name, year

      where s.state=f.state and f.ethnicity='T' 
      and f.week=52 and year in (2012, 2013)
  ) group by year, region_name
),
sick as (

    where year in (2013,2014) group by year, region
    )
  )
select sick.yoy_change, sick.region, sick.year as sick_year,

from sick, shots where sick.region=shots.region_name
and sick.year = (shots.year+1)

Building models¶

Both factors -- eligiblity and percentage vaccinated -- may have an effect on the response, but not visually apparent. We can build a linear model and see if it explains the data well. First we'll use the ols method in statsmodels to build a model that effectively states:

Year-over-year change in flu is caused by the total number of eligible medicare recipients, the percentage of recipients vaccinated, and the percentage vaccinated multipled by the number of eligible.

While this model is not likely accurate, the diagnostics on the model can give us insight into whether the factors explain any of the data.

In [8]: 
shots_to_sick = add_constant(shots_to_sick)
shot_to_sick_model = ols("yoy_change ~ all_eligible*vaccinated", data=shots_to_sick).fit()

The diagnostic information contains a few interesting pieces:

  • R-squared is only 0.309, which suggests that the model only explains 30% of the data
  • No factors have a p-value (P>|t|) of less than or equal to 0.05, which suggests that none of them has a significant effect

Basically, our model is wrong. This means we should ask another question: do we have the right factors to expliain the data?

Another Model¶

Suppose we consider a different factor. Perhaps the year-over-year change in flu cases is explained by access to flu shots, not strictly the increase in vaccinations. In this case, the factor of interest would be the change in elegibility.

Let's construct a new dataset to examine that scenario.

In [10]: 
%%sql with shots as (
  select all_eligible- lag(all_eligible) over (order by year) as eligible_change, region_name, year 
  from ( 
    select sum(f.eligible) as all_eligible, s.region_name, f.year
      from flushots f, state_stats s 
      where s.state=f.state and f.ethnicity='T' 
      and f.week=52 and year in (2012, 2013)
      group by year, region_name
  ) 
),
sick as (
  select total_a - lag(total_a) over (order by year) as yoy_change, region, year
  from (
    select sum(a_total) as total_a, region, year
    from us_who_flu_stats 
    where year in (2013,2014) group by year, region
    )
  )
select sick.yoy_change, sick.region, sick.year as sick_year, 
shots.year as shot_year, shots.eligible_change
from sick, shots where sick.region=shots.region_name
and sick.year = (shots.year+1)

Now we'll build another least squares model, this time the model states:

  • Year-over-year change in flu is caused by the year-over-year change in eligible medicare recipients

As we see in the summary, the R-squared values suggests that we're explaining more of the data with fewer parameters. Our new factor is also significant, as it has a p-value of less than 0.05. It suggests we're on the right track.

One way to check the goodness of fit for a model is to consider a plot of the residuals. Residuals are the difference between the value predicted by the model and the actual value observed in the dataset. Large residuals suggest a model which is incorrect in some way.

In this case, we'll look at a "component plus residual plot." The blue line gives us the predicted values, while the dots are the predicted values plus the residual (i.e., the original value). Note that the majority of the dots lie near the line.

Overall, the model is better and we're on the right track. However, change in Medicare eligibility isn't the only factor in reducing flu nationwide. Could change in percentage vaccinating year-over-year be significant? Are there other factors to consider?

Summary¶

We've taken our first step into modeling data and asking questions in the form of linear relationships. In this exercise we:

  • used analytical SQL and the lag function to find row-to-row differences
  • used scatter plots to probe for potential relationships in data
  • used ordinary least squares models to test hypotheses about relationships in data
  • used residual plots and summary tables to understand the quality of our models

How Do Sanitation and Clean Water Effect Flu Transmission?¶

The conditions in which we live have profound effects on our health. Just as the percentage of a country's population which is urban may impact flu transmission, sanitation and availability of clean water may play a role in how flu is spread.

In this exercise, we'll examine the effect of improvements in sanitation and potable water accessibility improvement on the flu. We'll do this by examining groups of data in parallel, modeling the most likely candidate, and exploring the concept of logistic regression.

To begin, we'll need our standard set of imports and a new one from scikit-learn.

While the effects are statistically significant, the relationship explained in the data -- and more pronouncedly in the residual plots -- suggests that the relationship is not linear. Instead, most of our residual points are clustered around a single area, and there are a few outliers. With more data, the model may be more meaningful, but we can't conclude anything with so little data.

However, looking at the data, it could be that rather than a linear effect on the data, there's a binary one. That is, perhaps sanitation and water supply improvements divide AFRO countries into 2 groups. One way we can examine this is using a logistic regression.

We still don't have enough data to form a significant model, but we can construct a logistic regression model to understand the concept.

We're going to be using logistic regression in a supervised fashion. That is, we need to provide the "right" answers for a set of data which will train our model. We can then use other data to test our model. This training-testing cycle is typical in machine learning applications.
